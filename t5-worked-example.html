Week 4

🏗️ What is an ADR — and why does it matter for your career?

An Architecture Decision Record (ADR) is a short document that captures a significant technical decision you made — what you decided, what you considered, and why you chose it over the alternatives.

The analogy: Think of it like a sat-nav journey log. You decided to take the M25 to get to your destination. The ADR records: "We chose the M25 (faster, reliable). We considered the A3 (scenic but slow) and the train (no flexibility). We accepted the risk of motorway traffic because speed was the priority." Six months later, when your colleague asks "why didn't we take the train?", the ADR answers it — without a meeting.

Why it separates Architects from Engineers: Anyone can build a solution. An Architect can explain and defend why that solution was chosen over everything else. That's what gets you promoted.

📄 T4 — Initiative Pitch 📐 T5 — Architecture Decision Record
What is it? A persuasion document. You're selling your initiative to your manager or team. A decision log. The decision has been made — now you're recording it for the record.
Who reads it? Your manager, stakeholders you need buy-in from. Your team, future engineers, your promotion panel — anyone who needs to understand why.
Tone "Here's what I propose and why you should approve it." "Here's what was decided, what was considered, and what we accepted as a trade-off."
Timing Before starting. You're getting alignment. As you make significant technical choices. Ongoing throughout the initiative.
The analogy The planning meeting where you propose which route to take. The journey log written after the decision — so no one debates the route again.
Career signal Shows strategic thinking and stakeholder awareness. Shows architectural thinking and professional maturity. This is Staff-level behaviour.
✅ Filled Example — Read This First

ADR-001: UI Test Automation Framework Selection

Chakri  ·  Echo Securities  ·  April 2026
✓ Accepted
Context
Echo Securities is migrating its core dashboard UI from an older AngularJS app to a modern React-based interface. The current QA process is entirely manual — no UI automation exists. The engineering team has been given a 6-month delivery timeline and expects the QA function to own a scalable automated regression suite that can be maintained by the QE team without developer involvement. The manager has explicitly asked for a Staff-level initiative that demonstrates architectural ownership.
Decision
We will adopt Playwright (TypeScript) as the UI test automation framework for the Echo Securities dashboard migration project.
Alternatives Considered
Option Pros Cons Verdict
Playwright (TS) CHOSEN Native async support, multi-browser, auto-wait, strong TypeScript support, active community, excellent CI integration Team unfamiliar — learning curve in weeks 1–2 Best long-term ROI; manager-recommended
Selenium (Java) REJECTED Widely known, extensive documentation, team has prior exposure Verbose setup, slower execution, explicit waits required, brittle on React apps Legacy choice — does not meet Staff-level expectations
Cypress REJECTED Good DX, fast feedback loops, good docs JavaScript-only, limited multi-tab support, no native cross-browser, licensing changes in 2024 Narrower use case; Playwright supersedes it for this context
Manual testing only REJECTED Zero upfront investment Not scalable for 6-month delivery cadence; no architectural ownership signal Not viable given programme and project goals
Decision Rationale
Playwright was prioritised because: (1) it is the framework explicitly suggested by the engineering manager, signalling team-wide alignment; (2) it natively handles React's async rendering without brittle explicit waits; (3) it supports TypeScript out of the box, aligning with the team's existing codebase language; (4) it integrates cleanly into GitHub Actions CI, enabling shift-left quality from sprint 1. The learning curve risk was mitigated by scheduling a 2-week ramp-up spike before writing production tests.
Consequences
Positive: Automated regression suite in place by sprint 4. Reduced manual testing cycle by ~60%. Foundation for future visual testing and API contract testing layers.

Accepted trade-off: 2-week learning investment required. First sprint's test output will be lower than steady-state.

If this decision proves wrong: Cypress remains a viable fallback, but migration cost would be ~1 sprint of rework. Decision to be reviewed at Week 6 if adoption is slower than expected.
Status
Accepted — Agreed with engineering manager in Week 3 1:1. Implementation started Week 4.
📌 Coach note: Notice what this ADR does — it proves Chakri thought like an Architect, not just a tester who picked a tool. It shows he evaluated options, considered trade-offs, and made a documented decision. That's the evidence a promotion panel needs. This document now lives in the repo — permanently. Your name is on it.
