The Initiative Pivot Playbook

When your situation changes, your initiative adapts — you don't start over.

How to use this: Fill in each section when your work situation changes — you've lost a project, been benched, changed teams, or your original initiative is no longer viable. The goal is to pivot your initiative within 48 hours, not abandon it. This keeps your momentum and your case study intact. Review with your coach before acting on the pivot.
When to use this template: Use it if any of these apply to you right now:
  • You've been benched or your project has ended
  • You've been moved to a different team or project
  • Your manager or sponsor has left the company
  • Your company has frozen new initiatives or imposed a hiring/spending freeze
  • You're actively job hunting and may leave your current company
  • Your original initiative is blocked for reasons outside your control
  • Your initiative is under political, HR, or structural risk — you need to shrink scope and go quieter
  • Life has intervened (health, family, workload) and you need to pause the programme cleanly

Part 2 — Choose Your Pivot Path

Based on your situation, pick the path that fits. You don't need to start from zero — you're redirecting, not restarting.

Path A — Same Company, Different Project

You're still employed but your project changed. Your initiative adapts to the new project.

  • Take your original initiative (e.g., "API Performance Monitoring Framework") and apply it to the new project
  • Your T4 pitch stays 80% the same — update the project name, stakeholders, and timeline
  • Your skills and approach transfer — only the context changes
  • Action: Update T4 within 48 hours. Identify new stakeholders. Continue building.
Path B — Benched / No Project

You have no active project. This is actually an opportunity — you have time to build.

  • Use the bench time to build a working POC/MVP of your initiative using open-source or sample projects
  • Frame it as "self-investment during bench time" — companies expect this
  • When a new project comes, you arrive with a working framework ready to deploy — instant visibility
  • Action: Start the AI Coaching Card from Phase 2 (LEARN). Build with a sample app. Document everything.
Path C — Job Hunting / Leaving Soon

You're actively looking for a new role. Your initiative becomes your interview case study.

  • Shift focus from "internal visibility" to "external proof"
  • Build a portfolio piece: a working automation framework on a public repo (sanitised, no company data)
  • Use templates T4, T5, T11 as your interview talking points — "Here's how I identified the problem, made the decision, and delivered the result"
  • Your case study becomes: "At my previous company, I identified that our release cycle was bottlenecked by manual regression. I proposed and built a test automation framework that reduced testing time by 40%."
  • Action: Create a public GitHub repo. Build the POC there. Write the case study (T11) for your portfolio.
Path D — Sponsor/Manager Left

Your initiative is the same but your champion is gone.

  • Identify the new decision-maker or stakeholder
  • Don't pitch from scratch — show progress: "I've been working on X, here's what I've achieved so far"
  • Results speak louder than proposals when you've lost your champion
  • Action: Update T6 (Stakeholder Map) with new names. Lead with results, not the pitch.
Path E — Scope at Risk

You're employed but political, HR, or structural instability is threatening your initiative. The work is still possible — it just has to become quieter and smaller.

  • De-scope publicly. Cut scope by 50% or more — make it smaller than anyone would object to.
  • Build privately first. Ship internal-only prototypes before anything visible to leadership.
  • Strip every programme reference from work artefacts. No template numbers, no coach name, no external frameworks.
  • Re-baseline stakeholders. If political sensitivity caused the risk, narrow the stakeholder list aggressively.
  • Action: Revised T4 with smaller scope within 48 hours. Private T11 draft continues in parallel so your case study survives whatever happens at work.
Path F — Paused

Life happened — health, family, workload crunch, or a gap you need. Pausing honestly is far better than pretending to be on-track.

  • Name the pause explicitly to your coach. No ghosting, no "I'll catch up next week" for weeks in a row.
  • Set a return date. A pause without a return date becomes a silent exit.
  • No delivery is expected in this state. Templates are skipped, not deferred.
  • On return, you resume at the phase you paused in — not where the cohort is.
  • Action: Tell your coach the pause reason and the return date. Put "PAUSED" against your Pivot Path in the tracker. Re-engage through a single 1:1, not a group call.
